💡 Alaska Cruise Pro Tips
12 cruises in — plus a few Alaska-specific ones
🧅Layer everything. Temps swing 20°F+ from morning to afternoon. Base layer, fleece, waterproof shell — bring all three every port day.
☂️Rain gear is non-negotiable. Even Skagway (the driest port) can surprise you. Ketchikan averages 160 inches of rain per year — pack ponchos for the kids.
🌅Eye masks for sleeping. In June you'll have 19 hours of daylight. It won't get dark. Blackout curtains help, but an eye mask is your friend.
🔭Binoculars on deck at all times. Humpbacks, orcas, bald eagles — they appear when you least expect it. Keep them accessible, not in the stateroom.
🛳️Your verandah is gold. Alaska is one of the best routes for verandah use — scenic cruising, wildlife from bed, glacier day views. Use it constantly.
📸Glacier Day character photo. Mickey & Minnie appear in their exclusive Alaska outfits. Best window is when the ship first enters Tracy Arm — be on deck early.
🗺️Watch the navigation screen. Deck 4 lobby has a live ship position map. Kids love watching it update as you move through the Inside Passage.
👟Waterproof shoes every port. Docks, gangways, and trails are wet. Leave the white sneakers for sea days.
🧲Bring magnetic hooks. Stateroom walls are magnetic — hooks let you hang wet layers and gear without cluttering the small bathroom.
👕Don't over-pack clothes. Laundry rooms are on board. Pack fewer outfits, wash mid-cruise, and leave room in your bag for Alaska souvenirs.

History

Gold was discovered in Canada's Yukon in 1896. Word reached the outside world in 1897 and 100,000 people stampeded north. Skagway went from a single homestead to Alaska's largest city — population 10,000 — almost overnight. Most prospectors found nothing. The White Pass trail you're riding was called the "Dead Horse Trail" because so many pack animals died on it. Skagway's entire historic downtown is now a National Historical Park.

History

Gold was discovered here in 1880 by prospectors Joe Juneau and Richard Harris — the city is named after Joe. Juneau became Alaska's capital in 1906. It's ringed by the Tongass National Forest (the largest in the US) and the Coast Mountain Range, making road construction impossible. Every Alaskan governor must fly or take a boat to the capital. The Mendenhall Glacier, just 12 miles from downtown, has been retreating since the 1700s and has lost over 1.75 miles since 1958.

History & Culture

The name Ketchikan comes from the Tlingit word Kitschk-Hin — "Thundering Wings of an Eagle." Tlingit, Haida, and Tsimshian peoples have lived here for thousands of years. Ketchikan grew as a salmon cannery hub in the late 1800s — at its peak, 13 canneries operated along the creek. Today it's home to the world's largest collection of standing totem poles: over 80 poles scattered across the city, each one a carved family history told in cedar.

Totem poles are like giant comic books carved in cedar! Each figure tells part of a story — a family's history, an important event, or a legend. They're read from bottom to top. Did You Know?
When a chunk of glacier breaks off, it's called "calving" — the sound can be as loud as a cannon. The brilliant blue color comes from ancient ice so compressed it absorbs all colors except blue.

Tracy Arm Fjord

Tracy Arm is a 30-mile-long fjord carved by glaciers over thousands of years. At its head sit Sawyer and South Sawyer glaciers — massive rivers of ice that flow all the way to the sea. The Disney Wonder will navigate between iceberg-dotted waters with sheer granite walls rising 3,000 feet on both sides. The glacier ice you'll see is often electric blue — so old and compressed that it absorbs every wavelength of light except blue. When a house-sized chunk calves off into the water, the sound echoes off the canyon walls like a thunderclap.

🧒 Kids Corner
Harbor seals actually give birth on the floating icebergs inside Tracy Arm! When the ship gets close, look for little seal pups resting on the ice floes. They use the icebergs as floating nurseries — safe from predators in the water below.
Deck tips for Glacier Day:
Layer up — it can be 20°F colder near the ice. The bow and upper decks give the best unobstructed views. Bring binoculars. Blue-tinted bergs are the oldest and most beautiful. Hot cocoa from the buffet hits different at the glacier. 🧤

History & Culture
⛏️
The Klondike Gold Rush (1896–1899)
Gold was discovered in Canada's Yukon Territory in 1896. Over 100,000 prospectors flooded through Skagway. Most found nothing. The White Pass route you're taking was the "easier" of two treacherous mountain trails.
🏛️
Tlingit, Haida & Tsimshian Peoples
Southeast Alaska has been home to Indigenous peoples for over 10,000 years. The totem poles in Ketchikan are Tlingit and Haida art — each tells a story about family, history, and the natural world, meant to be read top to bottom like a visual family history carved in cedar.
🧊
How Glaciers Form
Glaciers form when snow accumulates faster than it melts over hundreds of years. The weight compresses snow into dense blue-tinted ice. A tidewater glacier flows all the way to the ocean, where the ends "calve" in spectacular crashes. Mendenhall Glacier retreats about 100–150 feet per year.
🐟
The Salmon Cycle
Alaska's five salmon species are born in freshwater, migrate to the ocean, then return to the exact stream where they were born to spawn — and die. Their bodies feed the entire rainforest ecosystem. June is the start of salmon season in Ketchikan.
🌟
Alaska by the Numbers
Largest US state (twice the size of Texas). 3 million lakes. More coastline than all other US states combined. 17 of the 20 highest US peaks. In June, expect daylight until 10 PM or later — pack an eye mask.
